Part 1: Advanced Customer Scripts

These are detailed, word-for-word scripts for specific situations that are NOT covered in the basic Review Capture Scripts file.

SCENARIO 1: THE CUSTOMER WHO MENTIONS A COMPETITOR'S LOWER PRICE
Context:

During the sales consultation, the customer says, "Your price is higher than [Competitor]." You want to use reviews to justify the price difference.

Script:
"I totally get it—you want to make sure you're making the right choice. Here's the thing: we're not the cheapest, and we're not trying to be. But we are the best value. And here's how you know: we have over 200 Google reviews with a 4.9-star rating. [Competitor] has 40 reviews and a 4.2-star rating. Our customers are telling you something—they're saying we do what we promise, we show up on time, and we make the process smooth. That's worth a few extra dollars, especially when you're living with this floor for the next 15 years. You're not just buying a floor—you're buying peace of mind." [Pause and let them think] "I'd encourage you to read a few of our reviews and compare them to [Competitor]'s. I think you'll see the difference."
Why this works:
  • You're not being defensive about price.
  • You're reframing the conversation from "cheapest" to "best value."
  • You're using reviews as third-party proof that backs up your claim.

Part 2: Manager Coaching Scripts

These are scripts for managers to use when coaching team members who are struggling to ask for reviews.

Note: For scripts to address customer objections during the post-installation call, see the Review Capture Scripts – Manager Edition file (Script 8: Handling Internal Team Objections). The scripts below focus on 1-on-1 coaching conversations with individual team members.

SCENARIO 2: A SALESPERSON SAYS "I DON'T WANT TO BE PUSHY"
Context:

One of your salespeople is hesitant to ask for reviews because they don't want to annoy customers.

Script:
"I get it. But here's the thing: you're not being pushy. You're giving the customer an opportunity to help the next family who's trying to decide where to go. That's not pushy—that's generous. And here's the reality: if we don't ask, we don't get. And if we don't get reviews, we spend more money on ads, which means less money for raises, bonuses, and benefits. So asking for reviews isn't just good for the company—it's good for you. Let's practice the script together so it feels more natural. I'll be the customer. You call me and ask how the install went. Go."
Why this works:
  • You're reframing the ask from "pushy" to "generous."
  • You're connecting it to their paycheck (which makes it personal).
  • You're offering to practice with them (which builds confidence).

SCENARIO 3: A SALESPERSON SAYS "WHAT IF THEY LEAVE A BAD REVIEW?"
Context:

One of your salespeople is afraid to ask for reviews because they're worried the customer will leave a negative review.

Script:
"That's a valid concern. But here's the reality: the post-installation call is your insurance policy against bad reviews. When you call the customer 24–48 hours after the job, you're catching small issues early and fixing them before they turn into big problems. And here's the thing: most customers are happy. They're just waiting for someone to ask them to leave a review. Plus, the best defense against a bad review is volume. If you have 150 five-star reviews and one two-star review, nobody cares about the two-star review. But if you only have 20 reviews total, one bad one kills you. So the question isn't 'What if we get a bad review?' The question is 'What if we don't ask and we stay stuck at 20 reviews while our competitor climbs to 200?'"
Why this works:
  • You're addressing their fear directly (not dismissing it).
  • You're showing them how the post-installation call prevents bad reviews.
  • You're reframing the risk (staying stuck is worse than getting one bad review).
SCENARIO 4: A SALESPERSON SAYS "I'M TOO BUSY"
Context:

One of your salespeople says they don't have time to make post-installation calls or ask for reviews.

Script:
"I hear you. But here's the thing: the post-installation call takes 2 minutes. The text takes 30 seconds. That's less time than it takes to follow up on a cold lead three times. And every review you capture brings us more warm leads next month—which makes your job easier. So you're not too busy. You're investing 3 minutes now to save yourself hours later. Let's make it automatic. Set a reminder on your phone for 24 hours after every install. That way, you won't forget. And if you're really slammed, I can help you make a few of the calls this week. Sound good?"
Why this works:
  • You're showing them the ROI (3 minutes now = easier sales later).
  • You're offering a solution (set a reminder).
  • You're offering to help (which shows you're not just criticizing).

Part 3: Role-Play Scenarios for Team Training

These are exercises you can run during team meetings to practice the scripts and build confidence.

Note: For the basic scripts your team will practice, refer to the Review Capture Scripts file. The scenarios below show you how to structure role-play exercises and what to focus on during training.

ROLE-PLAY 1: THE HAPPY CUSTOMER
Setup:

One person plays the salesperson. Another person plays a happy customer who just had their floor installed.

Scenario:

The salesperson calls the customer 24–48 hours after installation to check in and ask for a review.

Script Reference:

Use Script 2: The Post-Installation Call (If Everything Went Well) from the Review Capture Scripts file.

Debrief (After the Role-Play):
  • Ask the team: "What did the salesperson do well?"
  • Ask: "What could they improve?"
  • Practice it 2–3 more times with different team members.
ROLE-PLAY 2: THE CUSTOMER WHO SAYS "I DON'T DO REVIEWS"
Setup:

One person plays the salesperson. Another person plays a customer who's hesitant to leave a review.

Scenario:

The salesperson calls the customer to check in, and the customer says, "I don't really do reviews."

Script Reference:

Use Script 5: Handling Customer Objections → Objection #1 from the Review Capture Scripts file.

Debrief (After the Role-Play):
  • Ask the team: "How did the salesperson handle the objection?"
  • Ask: "What made the customer feel comfortable?"
  • Practice it 2–3 more times with different scenarios.

ROLE-PLAY 3: THE CUSTOMER WHO HAD A SMALL ISSUE
Setup:

One person plays the salesperson. Another person plays a customer who mentioned a small issue (e.g., a crooked transition strip).

Scenario:

The salesperson calls to follow up after the issue was fixed.

Script Reference:

Use Script 7: Asking After You've Fixed a Problem from the Review Capture Scripts file.

Debrief (After the Role-Play):
  • Ask the team: "Why is this a powerful script?"
  • Answer: "Because it turns a potential negative into a positive. Customers often leave glowing reviews after you fix a problem—because it shows you care."
ROLE-PLAY 4: THE CUSTOMER WHO'S NOT TECH-SAVVY
Setup:

One person plays the salesperson. Another person plays an elderly customer who's not comfortable with technology.

Scenario:

The customer says, "I don't know how to do that."

Script Reference:

Use Script 5: Handling Customer Objections → Objection #3 from the Review Capture Scripts file.

Debrief (After the Role-Play):
  • Ask the team: "How did the salesperson make the customer feel comfortable?"
  • Answer: "They offered to help, and they gave the customer an out (ask a family member). That reduced anxiety."

Part 4: Quick Reference Micro-Scripts

These are short, powerful one-liners you can use in various situations.

When a Customer Says "I Already Told My Friends About You":

"That's awesome—thank you! And a Google review is kind of like telling a hundred friends at once. If you have a minute, we'd really appreciate it!"

When a Customer Says "I'll Do It Later":

"Perfect! I'm going to text you the link right now so you have it whenever you're ready. It only takes about 60 seconds. Thanks!"

When a Customer Hesitates:

"No pressure at all! If you get a chance, we'd love it. But either way, thanks so much for choosing us."

When You've Fixed a Problem:

"I'm really glad we could make it right. If you're comfortable with it, a review would actually help other people know that we stand behind our work. I'm going to text you the link—no pressure, though!"

When a Customer Says "I Don't Have Time":

"I totally get it. It only takes about 60 seconds—just rate us with stars and write a quick sentence. But no worries if you're too busy. Thanks again!"

Part 5: Team Training Meeting Format (20-Minute Role-Play Session)

Here's how to run a role-play training session at your weekly sales meeting.

Agenda (20 Minutes Total)

1. Introduce the Scenario (2 minutes)

What to say:

"Alright, team, we're going to practice the post-installation call today. I want everyone to feel confident with this script. Here's the scenario: The customer just had their floor installed 24 hours ago. You're calling to check in and ask for a review. Who wants to go first?"

2. First Role-Play (3 minutes)
  • One person plays the salesperson.
  • Another person (or you, the manager) plays the customer.
  • Run through the script from start to finish.
3. Team Feedback (2 minutes)

Ask the team:

  • "What did [Salesperson] do well?"
  • "What could they improve?"
4. Second Role-Play with a Twist (3 minutes)
  • Same setup, but this time the customer says, "I don't do reviews."
  • The salesperson practices handling the objection.

5. Team Feedback (2 minutes)

Ask the team:

  • "How did they handle the objection?"
  • "What made the customer feel comfortable?"
6. Third Role-Play (3 minutes)
  • Same setup, but this time the customer had a small issue that was fixed.
  • The salesperson practices asking for a review after resolving the issue.
7. Wrap-Up and Takeaways (5 minutes)

What to say:

"Great job, everyone. Here are the key takeaways: • Make the post-installation call 24–48 hours after the job. This prevents 'Silent Death' and sets you up for a positive review. • Send the text immediately—don't wait. • If the customer hesitates, reframe the ask as 'paying it forward' and offer no pressure. • If you fixed a problem, ask for a review—it shows you stand behind your work. Alright, let's go out there and capture some reviews this week. Who's with me?"
