REVIEW CAPTURE SCRIPTS – MANAGER EDITION

Complete Playbook: Customer Scripts + Team Coaching

Introduction for Managers

This document contains two critical parts:

Part 1: The exact customer-facing scripts your team should use to capture Google reviews at every stage of the customer journey (Scripts 1–7). These are the same scripts your salespeople will receive.

Part 2: Internal team management scripts and rollout guidance (Script 8 + implementation plan). This section is for managers only—do not distribute it to your sales team.

Why keep them separate? Because your salespeople need clear, confident scripts they can trust and use. They don't need to see you preparing for their objections in advance—that undermines trust and makes the system feel top-down instead of collaborative.

Use Part 1 to train your team. Use Part 2 to coach resisters, troubleshoot adoption challenges, and hold people accountable.

PART 1: CUSTOMER-FACING SCRIPTS

SCRIPT 1: Planting the Seed (During the Initial Consultation)

When to use: During or at the end of the sales consultation, before the customer signs.

Purpose: Set the expectation that you'll ask for a review later—so it's not a surprise.

"By the way, one of the ways we've grown is through word of mouth and online reviews. If we do a great job for you—and I'm confident we will—I'll ask you to leave us a quick review on Google. Fair enough?"
Why this works:
  • It's conditional ("if we do a great job"), which builds confidence.
  • It removes the surprise factor—the customer already agreed.
  • It positions your company as successful and review-driven (social proof).

Time investment: 10 seconds.

SCRIPT 2: The Post-Installation Call (24–48 Hours After the Job)

When to use: 24–48 hours after installation is complete.

Purpose: Check for "Silent Death" issues (small problems the customer hasn't reported), fix them fast, and ask for the review while the positive experience is fresh.

Script (If Everything Went Well):

"Hi [Customer Name], this is [Your Name] from [Company]. I'm just calling to check in and make sure everything went smoothly with your installation. How's the floor looking? Any questions or concerns I can help with?"

[Customer says everything's great]

"That's great to hear! I'm so glad we could take care of you. Now, I know you're probably busy, but if you have a minute this week, I'd REALLY appreciate it if you could leave us a quick review on Google. It helps us out a ton and helps other people feel confident choosing us. I'm going to text you a link right now so it's easy—does that work?"

[Customer agrees]

"Perfect! You'll see the text in just a second. Thanks so much—we really appreciate you!"

Script (If There's a Small Issue):

"Hi [Customer Name], this is [Your Name] from [Company]. I'm just calling to check in and make sure everything went smoothly with your installation. How's the floor looking? Any questions or concerns I can help with?"

[Customer mentions a small issue—e.g., a transition strip is crooked, a spot needs touch-up, etc.]

"Thanks so much for letting me know. I really appreciate you telling me—that's exactly the kind of thing I want to catch early. I'm going to get that taken care of right away. Let me call you back once it's fixed, and then I'll send you that review link. Sound good?"

[Fix the issue, then call back]

"Hi [Customer Name], this is [Your Name] again. Just wanted to let you know we took care of that [issue]. Everything should be perfect now. If you're happy with how we handled it, I'd love it if you could leave us a quick review on Google. I'm going to text you the link right now. Does that work?"

Why this works:
  • You're demonstrating that you care about quality and follow-through (which makes them want to leave a good review).
  • You're catching issues early, before they escalate into bad reviews.
  • You're asking for the review while the positive experience is still fresh.

Time investment: 2 minutes per call.

SCRIPT 3: The Text Message (Sent Immediately After the Call)

When to use: Immediately after the post-installation call, while you're still on the phone or right after you hang up.

Purpose: Get the review link into the customer's hands so there's no friction.

Text Template (Standard Version):

"Hi [Customer Name], thanks again for choosing us! Here's the link to leave your review: [Google Review Link]. It'll only take a minute, and it really helps us out. We appreciate you!"

Text Template (Pay It Forward Version):

"Hi [Customer Name], thanks again for trusting us with your project! If you have a minute, we'd love it if you could leave us a quick Google review. When you do, you're helping the next family who's trying to decide where to go—kind of like paying it forward. Here's the link: [Google Review Link]. We really appreciate you!"
Why this works:
  • The link is in their hand. There's no friction.
  • They can do it right now if they want, or later when they have time.
  • The "pay it forward" framing makes it feel altruistic, not transactional.

Time investment: 30 seconds.

SCRIPT 4: The Follow-Up Text (3–5 Days Later)

When to use: If the customer hasn't left a review within 3–5 days of your initial text.

Purpose: Give them a gentle, helpful reminder.

"Hi [Customer Name], just checking in—did the link I sent work okay? I know things get busy, but if you get a chance, we'd love that review! Here's the link again: [Google Review Link]. Let me know if you have any trouble with it. Thanks!"
Why this works:
  • You're not saying, "Hey, you forgot." You're saying, "Did the link work?"
  • It's a helpful, low-pressure nudge that often gets results.
  • The actionable question ("did the link work?") prompts a response and reduces customer guilt.

Time investment: 30 seconds.

SCRIPT 5: Handling Customer Objections

Sometimes customers will hesitate or push back. Here's how to handle the most common objections.

Objection #1: "I don't really do reviews."

Response:
"I totally understand—not everyone does. But I'll tell you, it really does help us out. And it also helps the next family who's trying to decide where to go—kind of like paying it forward. If you have a minute, I'd really appreciate it. No pressure, though!"

Objection #2: "I'll do it later."

Response:
"That's great, thank you! I'm going to text you the link right now so you have it whenever you're ready. It only takes about 60 seconds. Thanks so much!"

[Send the text immediately. Then follow up 3–5 days later if they don't leave the review.]

Objection #3: "I'm not tech-savvy / I don't know how."

Response:
"No problem at all! It's really easy—I'll text you a link, and all you have to do is click it, rate us with stars, and write a sentence or two about your experience. If you have any trouble, just call me back and I'll walk you through it. Sound good?"

Objection #4: "What if something goes wrong later?"

Response:
"That's exactly why I'm calling you now—to make sure everything's perfect before we ask for a review. If anything comes up later, you can always call us and we'll take care of it. That's part of our commitment to you. But if everything looks good right now, I'd love it if you could leave us a review. Does that sound fair?"

Objection #5: "I already recommended you to my neighbor."

Response:
"That's awesome—thank you so much! And honestly, an online review is kind of like recommending us to a hundred neighbors at once. It helps other people find us the same way you did. If you have a minute, I'd really appreciate it!"

SCRIPT 6: Asking in Person (If the Customer Comes Back to Your Showroom)

When to use: If the customer stops by your showroom after the job is done (to pick up leftover materials, ask a question, etc.).

Purpose: Ask for the review face-to-face.

"Hey [Customer Name]! Great to see you. How's the floor looking? Everything holding up well?"

[Customer says yes]

"That's great! Hey, real quick—if you have a minute, I'd love it if you could leave us a Google review. It helps us out a ton. I can text you the link right now, or if you have your phone handy, I can pull it up for you. Which works better?"

Why this works:
  • You're asking in person, which is harder to say no to.
  • You're offering to make it easy (text the link or pull it up right there).

SCRIPT 7: Asking After You've Fixed a Problem

When to use: After you've resolved a small issue the customer reported.

Purpose: Turn a potential negative into a positive by showing you care and follow through.

"Hi [Customer Name], this is [Your Name] from [Company]. Just wanted to let you know we took care of that [issue you mentioned]. Everything should be perfect now. Are you happy with how we handled it?"

[Customer says yes]

"That's great to hear. I'm really glad we could make it right. Now, if you're comfortable with it, I'd love it if you could leave us a review on Google. It helps other people know that if something does come up, we'll take care of it fast. I'm going to text you the link right now. Does that work?"

Why this works:
  • Customers often leave glowing reviews after you fix a problem—because it shows you care.
  • You're framing the review as proof that you stand behind your work.

SCRIPT 8: Handling Internal Team Objections

Sometimes your team will push back on asking for reviews. Here's how to handle the three most common objections—with data, logic, and real-world framing that connects the review system to their paycheck and job satisfaction.

When to use these scripts: In one-on-one coaching conversations, team meetings (when someone voices resistance publicly), or when you're rolling out the system for the first time.

Tone: Direct, empathetic, but firm. You're not asking for permission—you're explaining why this matters and holding them accountable.

Objection #1: "I don't want to be pushy."

Manager Response:

"I get it. And here's the thing: you're not being pushy. You're giving the customer an opportunity to help the next family who's trying to decide where to go. That's not pushy—that's generous.

But here's the reality: if we don't ask, we don't get. And if we don't get reviews, we spend more money on ads—which means less money for raises, bonuses, and benefits. So asking for reviews isn't just good for the company—it's good for you.

And one more thing: you're already doing great work. The review is just the customer telling other people what you already know—that we did a great job. You're not asking them to lie. You're asking them to share the truth. That's not pushy. That's confidence."

Why this works:
  • You're reframing "pushy" as "generous" and "confident."
  • You're connecting reviews to their paycheck (raises, bonuses, benefits).
  • You're reminding them that they're asking customers to share the truth, not exaggerate.

Objection #2: "What if they leave a bad review?"

Manager Response:

"That's exactly why we make the post-installation call—to catch problems early and fix them before they turn into bad reviews. If you're calling every customer 24–48 hours after the job, you're going to catch 95% of issues while they're still easy to fix.

And if someone does leave a bad review, we'll respond professionally and make it right. But the best defense against bad reviews is volume—and that's what this system builds.

Here's the truth: right now, unhappy customers are already leaving bad reviews—we're just not asking the happy ones to leave good reviews. So we're fighting with one hand tied behind our back. This system fixes that.

And one more thing: if you're worried about bad reviews, that tells me you care about quality—which is exactly the kind of person I want on this team. So let's use that energy to make sure we're delivering great work and capturing great reviews."

Why this works:
  • You're addressing the fear head-on with a logical process (post-installation calls catch issues early).
  • You're reframing the fear as a quality-control strength.
  • You're explaining that volume of good reviews is the best defense against occasional bad ones.

Objection #3: "I'm too busy."

Manager Response:

"I hear you. But here's the thing: the post-installation call takes 2 minutes. The text takes 30 seconds. That's less time than it takes to follow up on a cold lead three times.

And every review you capture brings us more warm leads next month—which makes your job easier. So you're not too busy. You're investing 3 minutes now to save yourself hours later.

Here's the math: if you spend 3 minutes per customer asking for reviews, and we capture 40–60% of them, we'll triple our digital leads in 6 months. That means more warm leads, fewer cold calls, higher close rates, and bigger paychecks.

So the question isn't 'Am I too busy?' The question is 'Do I want to keep grinding on cold leads, or do I want to invest 3 minutes per job to make my life easier?'

And here's the reality: this isn't optional. This is part of the job now. Just like you call to confirm the install, you call to ask for the review. It's that simple."

Why this works:
  • You're breaking down the time investment (3 minutes total) and comparing it to the time they already spend on lower-value tasks.
  • You're connecting the review system to their paycheck and job satisfaction (more warm leads, less cold calling).
  • You're making it clear that this is now a non-negotiable part of the process.

How to Roll Out These Scripts to Your Team

Rolling out a new system isn't just about handing people a script and saying "go do it." You need to create buy-in, train the process, hold people accountable, and celebrate wins. Here's your step-by-step rollout plan.

Step 1: The Kickoff Meeting (30–45 minutes)

Goal: Explain why reviews matter, show the data, address objections proactively, and get verbal commitment from the team.

Meeting Agenda:
  • Start with the problem (5 minutes): "Right now, we're spending $X per month on ads to generate leads. But most of those leads are cold—they've never heard of us, they're shopping price, and they're skeptical. That makes your job harder."
  • Show the data (5 minutes): Share the three findings: digital leads tripled, 70% of phone calls came from Google Business Profile, 30–40% total lead growth during review-active months.
  • Explain the system (10 minutes): Walk through the 4-step process: plant the seed, post-installation call, text the link, follow-up.
  • Address objections proactively (10 minutes): Use the responses from Script 8 to address the three big objections (pushy, bad reviews, too busy).
  • Connect it to their paycheck (5 minutes): "Here's what this means for you: more reviews = more warm leads = higher close rates = bigger paychecks."
  • Get verbal commitment (5 minutes): Go around the room and get a verbal "yes" from each person.

Step 2: Role-Play Training (30 minutes)

Goal: Practice the scripts out loud so they feel natural and confident.

How to run it:
  • Pair people up (or do it as a group with you playing the customer).
  • Have them practice each script 2–3 times: Script 1: Planting the seed, Script 2: Post-installation call (both versions), Script 5: Handling customer objections
  • Give feedback on tone, pacing, and confidence.
  • Encourage them to adjust the wording slightly to match their voice—but don't let them rewrite the whole script.

Key point: Role-playing feels awkward, but it's critical. If they don't practice out loud, they won't use the scripts in the field.

Step 3: Build Accountability into the Process

Goal: Make asking for reviews a non-negotiable part of the job—not an "extra" thing they might forget.

How to do it:
  • Job Folder Accountability Stamp: Add a checklist stamp to every job folder with these items: ☐ Seed planted during consultation, ☐ Post-installation call made (date: ______), ☐ Review link texted (date: ______), ☐ Follow-up sent (if needed) (date: ______), ☐ Review captured? (Y/N)
  • Weekly check-ins: In your weekly sales meetings, ask each salesperson: "How many post-installation calls did you make this week?" "How many review links did you send?" "How many reviews did you capture?"
  • Track it publicly: Use the Weekly Sales Meeting Dashboard Template to display each salesperson's review capture rate on a leaderboard.

Step 4: Celebrate Wins Publicly

Goal: Create positive reinforcement and social proof that the system works.

How to do it:
  • In team meetings: Read new reviews out loud and recognize the salesperson who captured them.
  • Break room wall: Print and post new reviews on a "Wall of Fame" in the break room.
  • Bonuses/incentives: Consider a small bonus ($25–$50) for every review captured, or a bigger bonus for hitting monthly targets (e.g., "First person to 10 reviews this month gets $200").

Step 5: Coach the Resisters One-on-One

Goal: Address individual objections privately and hold people accountable.

When to do it: If someone consistently skips the post-installation call or doesn't send review links, pull them aside for a one-on-one conversation.

What to say:

"Hey [Name], I noticed you didn't make post-installation calls on your last three jobs. What's going on?"

[Listen to their objection—usually one of the three from Script 8.]

[Use the appropriate response from Script 8 to address it.]

"Here's the deal: this is part of the job now. It's not optional. Just like you confirm the install, you make the post-installation call. I need you on board with this. Can I count on you to do it moving forward?"

[Get a verbal commitment.]

"Great. I'm going to check in with you next week to see how it's going."

Key point: Be direct, empathetic, but firm. Don't let resisters drag the team down.

Step 6: Track and Report Results

Goal: Show the team that the system is working—and that their effort is paying off.

What to track:
  • Number of post-installation calls made per week
  • Number of review links sent per week
  • Number of reviews captured per week
  • Overall review count and average star rating
  • Lead volume from Google Business Profile (track in your CRM or spreadsheet)
How to report it:
  • Share results in weekly team meetings: "Great week, team—we captured 8 new reviews, and our digital leads are up 15% this month."
  • Post results on the break room wall or leaderboard.

Why this matters: When people see that their effort is working, they'll keep doing it.

Pro Tips for Managers

  • Lead by example. If you're not making post-installation calls and asking for reviews, your team won't either.
  • Don't let perfect be the enemy of good. If someone uses the script and tweaks the wording slightly, that's fine—as long as they're asking.
  • Address resistance early. If someone pushes back in a team meeting, address it immediately (using Script 8). Don't let it fester.
  • Celebrate small wins. Every review matters. Make a big deal out of each one, especially in the first 30 days.
  • Be patient but firm. It takes 30–60 days for the system to become a habit. Hold people accountable, but give them time to adjust.
