The 4-Step System to Capture Reviews (Without Being Pushy)

Okay, so reviews matter. But how do you actually get them—without annoying the customer or feeling awkward?

Here's the proven 4-step process.

Step 1: Plant the Seed Early (During the Sale)

Don't surprise the customer with a review request after the job is done. Set the expectation early—during your consultation—so they know it's coming.

Here's the script:

"By the way, one of the ways we've grown is through word of mouth and online reviews. If we do a great job for you—and I'm confident we will—I'll ask you to leave us a quick review on Google. Fair enough?"

Why this works:

  • It frames the review request as conditional on great service (which builds confidence).
  • It removes the surprise factor. The customer already agreed to it.
  • It positions your company as successful and review-driven (social proof).

Time investment: 10 seconds.

Step 2: Make the Post-Installation Call (24–48 Hours After the Job)

This is the most important step—and the one most salespeople skip.

Here's why it matters:

Not all unhappy customers complain. Some will smile, say everything's fine, and then quietly decide never to refer you—or worse, leave a bad review months later.

We call this "Silent Death." The customer who doesn't complain, but also doesn't come back or recommend you.

The post-installation call prevents Silent Death.

When you call 24–48 hours after the job is done, you're giving the customer a chance to tell you about any small issues (a crooked transition strip, a spot that needs touch-up) while they're still easy to fix.

Think of it like a punch list in construction. You catch the little stuff, fix it fast, and the customer is thrilled you cared enough to check.

This is the difference between a punch list and a problem:

  • A punch list is a small issue you catch early and fix fast. The customer appreciates it.
  • A problem is the same issue, ignored, that festers and turns into a bad review or lost referral.

Here's the script:

"Hi [Customer Name], this is [Your Name] from [Company]. I'm just calling to check in and make sure everything went smoothly with your installation. How's the floor looking? Any questions or concerns I can help with?"

If everything's great:

"That's great to hear! I'm so glad we could take care of you. Now, I know you're probably busy, but if you have a minute this week, I'd really appreciate it if you could leave us a quick review on Google. It helps us out a ton and helps other people feel confident choosing us. I'm going to text you a link right now so it's easy—does that work?"

If there's an issue:

"Thanks so much for letting me know. I'm going to get that taken care of right away. Let me call you back once it's fixed, and then I'll send you that review link. Sound good?"

Why this works:

  • You're demonstrating that you care about quality and follow-through (which makes them want to leave a good review).
  • You're catching issues early, before they escalate.
  • You're asking for the review while the positive experience is still fresh.

Time investment: 2 minutes per call.

Step 3: Text the Link Immediately (While You're Still on the Phone)

Don't wait. Don't say, "I'll send it later." Send it while you're talking to them.

Here's the text template:

"Hi [Customer Name], thanks again for choosing us! Here's the link to leave your review: [Google Review Link]. It'll only take a minute, and it really helps us out. We appreciate you!"

Alternative version (with "pay it forward" framing):

"Hi [Customer Name], thanks again for trusting us with your project! If you have a minute, we'd love it if you could leave us a quick Google review. When you do, you're helping the next family who's trying to decide where to go—kind of like paying it forward. Here's the link: [Google Review Link]. We really appreciate you!"

Why this works:

  • The link is in their hand. There's no friction.
  • They can do it right now if they want, or later when they have time.
  • You're framing it as helping future customers, not just helping you.

Time investment: 30 seconds.

Step 4: Follow Up (3–5 Days Later if They Haven't Left a Review)

Life gets busy. People forget. A friendly follow-up reminder is not pushy—it's helpful.

Here's the follow-up text:

"Hi [Customer Name], just checking in—did the link I sent work okay? I know things get busy, but if you get a chance, we'd love that review! Here's the link again: [link]. Let me know if you have any trouble with it. Thanks!"

Why this works:

  • You're not saying, "Hey, you forgot." You're saying, "Did the link work?"
  • It's a helpful, low-pressure nudge that often gets results.

Time investment: 30 seconds.

Total Time Per Customer: 3 Minutes, 10 Seconds

That's it. That's the system.

If you do this consistently, you'll capture reviews from 40–60% of your customers.

And here's the payoff:

Every review you capture makes your job easier next month—because it brings more warm leads through the door.

How to Use Reviews to Close More Sales

Capturing reviews is only half the system. The other half is using them to close more deals.

Here's how.

Tactic #1: Ask "How Did You Hear About Us?" (And Use Their Answer)

When a customer walks in or calls, ask:

"How did you hear about us?"

If they say, "I found you on Google" or "I read your reviews," call it out:

"That's great! Yeah, we're really proud of our reviews. We work hard to take care of every customer, and it's nice to know people feel good enough about us to share their experience. We'll take great care of you too."

Why this works: You're reinforcing their decision to choose you. They already trust you—now you're validating that trust.

Tactic #2: Send Curated Reviews in Follow-Up Emails

After your initial consultation, send a follow-up email that includes 2–3 reviews that match the customer's specific concerns.

Example:

  • If they're worried about price, send a review that talks about value and affordability.
  • If they're concerned about installation quality, send a review that praises your installers.
  • If they're nervous about the process, send a review that talks about how easy and stress-free it was.

How to do this: Ask your manager for the Review Catalog (a document that organizes your best reviews by theme). Then pull the 2–3 that are most relevant.

Pro Tip: Don't just paste the review text. Write a sentence like, "I thought you might like to see what other customers said about [their concern]."

Tactic #3: Use Reviews to Handle Objections

When a customer says, "I'm getting quotes from a few other places," point them to your reviews.

"That makes total sense—you want to make sure you're making the right choice. One thing I'd encourage you to do is compare our Google reviews to theirs. We've got over 200 five-star reviews, and people talk a lot about our quality, our installers, and how we handle any issues that come up. That's not something every company can say. You're not just buying a floor—you're buying peace of mind."

Why this works: You're shifting the conversation from price to value. And reviews are third-party proof that backs up everything you're saying.

Tactic #4: Frame Risk (Because You Can't Resell a Floor)

Here's a line every customer needs to hear:

"You can't resell a floor. Once it's in, you're living with it for 10–15 years. So the real question isn't 'Who's cheapest?' It's 'Who's going to do it right the first time and be there if something goes wrong?' That's where our reviews matter. People don't just talk about our product—they talk about our service, our follow-through, and how we treat them after the sale."

Why this works: You're reframing the decision. It's not about saving $500 now. It's about avoiding a $5,000 mistake later.

Tactic #5: Build Your Own "About Me" Sheet with Your Best Reviews

If customers mention you by name in a review, ask your manager to pull those reviews and create a simple one-page "About Me" sheet for you.

What to include:

  • Your name and photo
  • A short intro (e.g., "I've been helping homeowners find the perfect flooring for 5 years, and I love making the process easy and stress-free.")
  • 3–5 reviews where customers mention you by name

How to use it: Keep it on your desk or hand it to customers during consultations.

Why this works: It builds credibility fast. Customers see that other people trusted you, liked working with you, and got great results.

Tactic #6: Use the Review Wall During Consultations

If your showroom has a review wall (physical or digital), use it during consultations.

When you're walking a customer through samples, gesture to the wall and say:

"By the way, if you want to see what other customers have said about us, we've got some of our best reviews right over there. People talk a lot about our installers and how clean and professional they are—that's something we're really proud of."

Why this works: It's a soft, conversational way to reinforce trust. You're not forcing them to read reviews—you're just pointing out that they're there.

Common Objections (And How to Handle Them)

Let's address the concerns you might have about asking for reviews.

Objection #1: "I don't want to be pushy."

Response:

You're not being pushy. You're giving the customer an opportunity to help the next family who's trying to decide where to go.

Frame it as "paying it forward," not begging for a favor.

"When you leave a review, you're not doing me a favor—you're helping the next family who's trying to decide where to go. You're basically paying it forward. That's not pushy. That's generous."

Objection #2: "What if they leave a bad review?"

Response:

If you're making the post-installation call (Step 2), you're catching problems early—before they turn into bad reviews.

And here's the reality: Most customers are happy. They just haven't been asked to leave a review yet.

Bottom line: The post-installation call is your insurance policy against bad reviews.

Objection #3: "I'm too busy."

Response:

The post-installation call takes 2 minutes. The text takes 30 seconds.

If you're too busy to spend 3 minutes capturing a review, you're too busy to make more money.

Because every review you capture brings you more warm leads next month.

Objection #4: "What if they say no?"

Response:

Most customers won't say no. And if they do, it's not personal.

Here's the reality: You'll get more "yeses" than "nos." And every "yes" brings you one step closer to more leads, easier sales, and higher commissions.
