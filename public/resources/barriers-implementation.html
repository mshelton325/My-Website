OVERCOMING BARRIERS TO IMPLEMENTATION

Solutions for the 14 Most Common Reasons Teams Don't Ask for Reviews

Introduction

The system is simple. The scripts work. The ROI is undeniable.

So why don't teams consistently ask for reviews?

Because asking for reviews feels uncomfortable—even when you know it works.

This document addresses the 14 most common barriers that prevent sales teams from asking for reviews consistently—and gives you (the manager) specific scripts, tools, and strategies to overcome each one.

Use this as your troubleshooting guide when someone on your team says:
  • "I don't want to be pushy."
  • "What if they leave a bad review?"
  • "I'm too busy."
For each barrier, you'll get:
1. The objection (what the salesperson says)
2. The root cause (what's really going on)
3. The fix (how to address it)
4. The script (exact words to say)

The 14 Barriers (And How to Fix Them)

BARRIER #1: "I DON'T WANT TO BE PUSHY"
What the salesperson says:

"I don't want to bother the customer. They just spent a lot of money with us. It feels pushy to ask for something else."

Root cause:

The salesperson thinks asking for a review is asking for a favor—when in reality, it's offering the customer a platform to share their experience.

The fix:

Reframe the ask as helping future customers, not helping yourself.

Script (for managers to use with the team):

"I get it. You don't want to be pushy. But here's the thing: when you ask for a review, you're not asking the customer to do you a favor. You're giving them a platform to help other people like them.

Mrs. Johnson was stressed out before she called us. She didn't know which company to trust. She spent an hour reading reviews before she picked up the phone.

Now that she's happy with her floors, don't you think she'd want to help the next person who's in her shoes?

You're not being pushy. You're offering her a way to pay it forward. That's the frame. Try it."

Pro Tip: Use the "Pay It Forward" text template from the Review Capture Scripts file. It reframes the ask as helping other customers, not promoting yourself.

BARRIER #2: "WHAT IF THEY LEAVE A BAD REVIEW?"
What the salesperson says:

"What if I ask for a review and they leave a 1-star review? I'd rather not ask at all."

Root cause:

Fear of negative feedback. The salesperson is worried they'll trigger a bad review that wouldn't have happened otherwise.

The fix:

Explain that the post-installation call prevents bad reviews—it doesn't cause them.

Script (for managers to use with the team):

"Let me ask you this: if a customer is unhappy, do you think ignoring them will make them less likely to leave a bad review?

Here's what actually happens: unhappy customers who don't get a follow-up call are MORE likely to leave a bad review—because they feel ignored.

The post-installation call does two things:

  • It catches small issues early (before they turn into big problems).
  • It shows the customer you care enough to follow up.

If there's a problem, you fix it. If everything went well, you ask for the review.

Either way, you're ahead. The customers who are going to leave a bad review were going to do it anyway—you're just getting in front of it now."

Pro Tip: Reference Part 7 of the Manager Manual: "The Manager's Dilemma" section explains the financial upside of proactive follow-up.

BARRIER #3: "I'M TOO BUSY"
What the salesperson says:

"I don't have time to call every customer after the install. I'm already swamped."

Root cause:

The salesperson doesn't see review capture as part of their job—they see it as extra work.

The fix:

Show them the time investment (3 minutes per customer) and the financial payoff (warmer leads = easier sales = less grinding).

Script (for managers to use with the team):

"I hear you. You're busy. But let's talk about what 'busy' actually means.

Right now, you're spending 8–10 hours a week chasing cold leads, doing multiple follow-ups, and convincing skeptical customers to trust us. That's exhausting.

What if I told you that for 3 minutes per customer—literally 3 minutes—you could cut that grind in half?

Here's the math:

  • Post-installation call: 2 minutes
  • Text the review link: 30 seconds
  • Follow-up text: 30 seconds
  • Total: 3 minutes, 10 seconds

If you do 10 jobs per month, that's 30 minutes of your time.

In exchange, you get warmer leads next month—leads who already trust us because they read your reviews. Those leads are easier to sell, they close faster, and they pay more.

So yes, you're 'busy.' But you're busy with the wrong things. Let's fix that."

Pro Tip: Block 15 minutes at the end of each day for post-installation calls. Treat it like a mandatory task—not an optional one.

BARRIER #4: "THE CUSTOMER ALREADY SEEMS ANNOYED—I DON'T WANT TO PUSH IT"
What the salesperson says:

"The customer seemed stressed during the consultation. I don't want to bother them again."

Root cause:

The salesperson is interpreting normal customer stress as dissatisfaction with the company—when in reality, the customer is just overwhelmed by the decision.

The fix:

Explain that the post-installation call is a relief for customers—not a burden.

Script (for managers to use with the team):

"Here's what you're missing: the customer isn't annoyed with you. They're stressed because flooring is a big decision and they're worried something will go wrong.

When you call them 24–48 hours after the install and say, 'Hey, I just wanted to check in—how did everything go?'—that's not annoying. That's reassuring.

Most companies don't call. Most companies disappear after the install. When you follow up, you're proving that we're different.

And here's the best part: if there IS a small issue, the customer is relieved that you asked—because now they don't have to figure out how to bring it up.

You're not bothering them. You're giving them permission to be honest."

BARRIER #5: "I KEEP FORGETTING TO MAKE THE CALL"
What the salesperson says:

"I always mean to call, but by the time I remember, it's too late."

Root cause:

There's no system or trigger to remind them.

The fix:

Build the callback into the workflow so it's automatic, not optional.

Script (for managers to use with the team):

"I get it. You're juggling a lot. So let's make this easier.

From now on, here's the rule: as soon as an install is completed, you set a reminder on your phone for 24–48 hours later. That's when you make the call.

Or, if you'd rather, I can set up a shared calendar where I assign you the callback as a task. Either way, we're building a system so you don't have to rely on memory."

Tool: The Job Folder Stamp

Another option: Use the Job Folder Accountability Stamp (see Bonus Tool below). Every job folder gets stamped with:

  • ☐ Post-installation call completed (Date: ______)
  • ☐ Customer feedback: ___________________________
  • ☐ Review requested? (Yes / No)
  • ☐ Review received? (Yes / No)

This creates a paper trail and makes it easy to see who's following the process.

BARRIER #6: "THE CUSTOMER SAID THEY'D DO IT, BUT THEY NEVER DID"
What the salesperson says:

"I asked for the review and the customer said yes, but they never left one. What do I do?"

Root cause:

The customer had good intentions but got distracted. They need a gentle nudge.

The fix:

Follow up 3–5 days later with a friendly text reminder.

Script (for managers to use with the team):

"This is totally normal. People get busy. They mean to leave the review, but they forget.

Here's what you do: 3–5 days after you send the link, follow up with a short text.

Use this template (from the Review Capture Scripts file):

'Hi [Customer Name], this is [Your Name] from [Company]. I just wanted to follow up—were you able to leave us that review? If not, no worries! Here's the link again: [Link]. Thanks so much!'

That's it. Simple, friendly, and effective. Most customers will leave the review after the follow-up."

Pro Tip: A 3–5 day follow-up text increases your response rate by 20–30%.

BARRIER #7: "THEY SAID THEY DON'T KNOW HOW TO LEAVE A REVIEW"
What the salesperson says:

"The customer said they're not tech-savvy and don't know how to leave a Google review."

Root cause:

The customer is intimidated by the process—especially if they're older or not comfortable with smartphones.

The fix:

Walk them through it step by step over the phone or in person.

Script (for managers to use with the team):

"No problem. Here's what you do: stay on the phone with them and walk them through it.

Say this:

'No worries, Mrs. Johnson—it's really easy. I'll walk you through it. First, open the text I just sent you and click the link. Do you see it?'

[Wait for her to click.]

'Great! Now you should see a screen that says "Write a review" with 5 stars at the top. Tap the number of stars you'd like to give us—hopefully 5! Then, just type a few sentences about your experience. It doesn't have to be long. When you're done, hit "Post."'

[Wait while she types.]

'Perfect! Thank you so much, Mrs. Johnson. I really appreciate it.'

Done. Most customers just need a little hand-holding."

Pro Tip: Offer to do this in person if the customer is in your showroom or if you're doing a final walkthrough at their home.

BARRIER #8: "I DON'T WANT TO INCENTIVIZE THEM—IT FEELS LIKE BRIBERY"
What the salesperson says:

"Isn't paying people to leave reviews against Google's rules? I don't want to get us in trouble."

Root cause:

Confusion about Google's review policies.

The fix:

Clarify the difference between incentivizing employees to ask for reviews (allowed) vs. paying customers to leave reviews (not allowed).

Script (for managers to use with the team):

"Great question. Let's clear this up.

You're right: it's against Google's rules to pay customers to leave reviews. We're not doing that.

What we ARE doing is paying YOU (the salesperson) to ask for reviews. That's totally fine.

Here's the difference:

❌ Not allowed: 'Mrs. Johnson, if you leave us a 5-star review, we'll give you a $50 gift card.'

✅ Allowed: 'Sarah (salesperson), for every verified Google review you capture, you'll earn $15–$20.'

We're incentivizing the team to ask—not incentivizing customers to post. Big difference."

BARRIER #9: "OUR INSTALLERS DON'T CARE ABOUT REVIEWS—THEY JUST WANT TO GET PAID"
What the salesperson says:

"The installers aren't bought in. They think reviews are 'a sales thing' and don't see how it benefits them."

Root cause:

Installers don't understand that reviews are a team effort—and that they benefit when the company gets more leads.

The fix:

Include installers in the incentive program and show them how reviews impact their income.

Script (for managers to use with the team):

"You're right. Installers need to see the 'why.'

Here's what you tell them:

'Every glowing review we get brings us more leads next month. More leads = more jobs. More jobs = more hours and more income for you. It's that simple.'

And here's how we're going to make it even more tangible: from now on, every time a customer mentions an installer by name in a review, that installer gets a $25 bonus (or $5-$10 per mention, depending on your budget).

Use the Installer Card System (90-Day Review Acceleration Plan, Week 6). Print business cards with the installer's name and photo, and have them hand the card to the customer at the end of the job with this script:

'If you're happy with how everything turned out, I'd really appreciate it if you could leave us a review and mention me by name. Here's my card with the link. Thanks!'

When installers see their name in a review—and get paid for it—they'll start caring."

BARRIER #10: "WE'VE TRIED THIS BEFORE AND IT DIDN'T WORK"
What the salesperson says:

"We asked for reviews last year and nobody left them. Why would it work now?"

Root cause:

The previous attempt was inconsistent, untracked, or poorly incentivized.

The fix:

Show them the difference between a one-off campaign and a systematic process.

Script (for managers to use with the team):

"I get it. You tried it before and it didn't work. Let me ask you this: did you have scripts? Did you have a tracking system? Did you have incentives? Did you follow up consistently?

Here's the difference: we're not just 'asking for reviews.' We're building a system.

This time, we have:

  • ✅ Proven scripts (tested on thousands of customers)
  • ✅ A tracking system (leaderboard + accountability)
  • ✅ Financial incentives ($15–$20 per review)
  • ✅ Weekly check-ins (so we can troubleshoot and improve)

This isn't a campaign. It's a permanent part of how we do business.

And here's the proof: stores that implement this system capture 40–60% of their reviews. That means if we do 20 jobs per month, we should capture 8–12 reviews. Every month. That's 96–144 reviews per year.

Let's give it 90 days and track the results. If it doesn't work, we'll adjust. But I'm betting it will."

BARRIER #11: "WHAT IF THE CUSTOMER SAYS, 'I DON'T LEAVE REVIEWS'?"
What the salesperson says:

"Some customers just flat-out say they don't leave reviews. What do I say?"

Root cause:

The customer doesn't see the value in leaving a review—or they're worried it will take too long.

The fix:

Use the "Pay It Forward" reframe and emphasize how quick it is.

Script (for managers to use with the team):

"When a customer says, 'I don't leave reviews,' here's what you say:

'I totally understand. Can I ask you this: before you called us, did you read any reviews about flooring companies online?'

[They'll probably say yes.]

'Right. So you know how helpful those reviews were when you were trying to figure out who to trust. That's all we're asking—just a quick sentence or two to help the next person who's in your shoes. It takes 60 seconds, and it really makes a difference. Here's the link.'

[Send the link immediately while they're still on the phone.]

Most customers will do it if you frame it as helping future customers—not promoting your business."

BARRIER #12: "I'M AFRAID OF SEEMING DESPERATE"
What the salesperson says:

"Won't asking for reviews make us look like we're begging for business?"

Root cause:

The salesperson thinks review requests signal weakness—when in reality, they signal confidence.

The fix:

Reframe it: asking for reviews is what high-performing companies do.

Script (for managers to use with the team):

"Let me flip that on you: do you think Amazon asks for reviews because they're desperate?

Of course not. They ask because they know reviews matter. Every high-performing company in the world asks for reviews.

When you ask a happy customer to share their experience, you're not begging. You're showing confidence. You're saying: 'We did great work, and we're proud enough to ask you to tell other people about it.'

The customers who are going to think you're desperate weren't going to leave a review anyway. The customers who are happy? They're honored you asked."

BARRIER #13: "OUR GOOGLE BUSINESS PROFILE ISN'T SET UP CORRECTLY"
What the salesperson says:

"I tried to generate the review link, but I don't think our Google profile is set up right."

Root cause:

The business doesn't have a verified Google Business Profile, or the profile isn't optimized.

The fix:

Prioritize this as a Week 1 task in the 90-Day Review Acceleration Plan.

Script (for managers to use with the team):

"Great catch. Let's fix that right now.

Step 1: Verify your Google Business Profile.

  • Go to google.com/business
  • Search for your business name
  • Claim your profile (if you haven't already)
  • Verify your business (Google will mail you a postcard with a verification code)

Step 2: Generate your Google review link.

  • Follow the instructions in the Review Request Text Templates file

Step 3: Test the link on your phone to make sure it works.

This should take 15–30 minutes total. Let's get it done this week."

And here's the kicker for you, as the owner: if you're paying yourself a percentage of revenue (or your sales manager is bonused on sales volume), this system actually increases your take-home income by $30,000+ per year—even though you're spending less on marketing.

How? Because your close rate, lead volume, and average ticket all go up. (See Part 7 of the Manager Manual: 'The Manager's Dilemma.')

This isn't an expense. It's the highest-ROI investment you can make."

Bonus Tool: The Job Folder Stamp

What it is:

A physical stamp (or printed checklist) that goes on every job folder after the installation is complete.

Why it works:

It creates accountability, gives you a paper trail, and makes it easy to see (at a glance) who's following the review capture process and who's not.

What the stamp says:

☐ Post-installation call completed Date: ______ Time: ______ ☐ Customer feedback: _________________________________ _________________________________ ☐ Review requested? (Yes / No) ☐ Review link sent via text? (Yes / No) ☐ Follow-up text sent (3–5 days later)? (Yes / No) ☐ Review received? (Yes / No) ☐ Customer mentioned installer by name? (Yes / No) Installer: _______________ ☐ Thank-you card sent? (Yes / No)
How to use it:
  1. Print this checklist on a rubber stamp or as a printed sticker.
  2. Every time an install is completed, the salesperson stamps the job folder.
  3. Within 24–48 hours, they make the post-installation call and fill out the stamp.
  4. At the end of the week, the manager reviews all job folders and checks:
  • Who's making the calls?
  • Who's capturing reviews?
  • Who's skipping steps?

Pro Tip: Use this stamp during 1-on-1 performance reviews. Pull out the last 10 job folders and ask: "Why are 7 of these missing the post-installation call? What's going on?"
